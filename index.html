00 Orientation Where this comes from

First — what a “forward deployed engineer” is.

It's a term from the software industry: someone a technology company sends to embed directly with a customer — not to sell a product, but to sit beside the people using it, learn the work, and tune the thing until it fits.

The role grew up in high-stakes enterprise software and is now central to the frontier AI labs — OpenAI and others field forward-deployed teams, half-jokingly making the labs deployment companies first and model-makers second. But that attention points almost entirely at the giants.

This proposal brings the same model down to your size, from an independent provider rather than a sales org: I get your team onto the right tools — say, a five-seat Claude Team plan — learn each person's work, then either build the tools for you or teach your staff to build them. The rest of this document is the how.

01 The gap Why this, why now

The barrier was never the technology.

When AI doesn't land at a small nonprofit, it's rarely about software. It's about the distance between a general-purpose tool and the specific, idiosyncratic way a particular team gets its work done. Four things tend to be said out loud:

Heard in the office — 01

We don't really know what's possible.

The headlines are about chatbots and lawsuits. Nobody has shown us what AI does for our intake forms, our grant calendar, our volunteer list.

Heard in the office — 02

We're afraid it's about replacing people.

Our staff are stretched thin and loyal. The last thing we want is to make anyone feel like the goal is to do their job without them.

Heard in the office — 03

We can't afford a tech team.

There's no budget line for a software developer, and the off-the-shelf platforms cost more than they save and still don't fit.

Heard in the office — 04

The generic tools don't fit how we work.

We've tried the all-in-one systems. We end up bending our work to the software instead of the other way around.

The missing piece isn't another tool. It's a person who understands both the tools and your work — and who builds the gap closed.

02 The model What a forward deployed engineer is

Borrowed from one of the hardest problems in software.

The “forward deployed engineer” emerged in live, high-stakes, constantly-changing software environments — the kind where slideware fails and only working software counts.

A forward deployed engineer doesn't sell you a product from across a table. They embed inside your team, learn the domain from the people who actually do the work, and build and adjust real software against your real problems — in the room, in real time.

But the model travels much further than its corporate origins suggest. A food bank, a land trust, a literacy program, a small arts nonprofit — each is a “dynamic, continuously evolving” operation with deep domain knowledge locked in its people and almost none of it written down. That is exactly the environment the role was built for.

The difference for you is the scale and the tone: not a sprawling enterprise rollout, but a patient hand that makes mission-driven people faster at the work they already love.

03 The reframe Augment, don't replace

Hand each person a power tool — not a pink slip.

The whole engagement rests on a promise that's easy to state and easy to keep: every tool is made to help a specific named person do the part of their job that only they can do — and to delete the part a computer should have been doing all along. The development director still decides which funders to court and how to tell the story. The program coordinator still runs the program. The drudgery around them is what goes.

So the multiplier up top is real, but it's honest: task by task, not a blanket claim over everything. The win that compounds is the hours handed back every single week — given to the mission instead of the paperwork. Nobody on staff becomes redundant. Everybody becomes harder to replace.

And replacement is really a big-company story — it happens where there's slack to cut: bloated headcounts, redundant layers, work nobody can quite explain.

A small foundation or grant agency is the opposite case. A handful of people each do the work of five, wearing ten hats, with no slack at all — so AI has nothing to subtract. It just hands the overstretched their time back. The fear of being replaced is borrowed from a kind of workplace that isn't yours.

04 Two ways in Build for you, or build your builders

You can hire the tool — or learn to make your own.

The starting point is identical: I sit with your team and build something real against your actual work. What happens after the build is the choice — and most teams end up wanting some of both.

Mode A · Done for you

I build it for you.

The embedded builder. You point at the work; I ship the tools. The fastest path to hours back — ideal when no one on staff has the bandwidth to learn the machinery right now.

  • Working prototypes, shaped to your team and handed over ready to use
  • Runs as a one-time sprint, or an ongoing contract as new needs surface
  • You get results now; the how stays under the hood until you want it
Best for: teams that want outcomes, not homework.
Mode B · Taught to you

I teach you to build it.

Same conversation, same build — then I open the hood. We look at exactly what the AI handed me, how I reshaped it into something worth trusting, and the best practice for that specific kind of build. Your staff leave able to make the next one.

05 The engagement How a deployment runs

Four moves, repeated until the tools are theirs.

A real sequence, not a flowchart on a wall. It starts with listening and ends with you owning everything that gets built.

PHASE 01

Listen

A one-on-one with every staff member. What's your week? What eats it? What do you dread? How comfortable are you with computers — honestly? No jargon, no quiz. Just the work, in their words.

Week 1
PHASE 02

Map

Find the handful of places where today's AI genuinely helps — and say plainly where it doesn't. A short, ranked list of prototypes worth building, each tied to a person and a number of hours.

Week 1–2
PHASE 03

Prototype

Build real, working tools in days — not decks, not roadmaps. Put each one in the hands of the person it's for, watch how they actually use it, and cut and reshape around what really happens.

Week 2–6
PHASE 04

Hand off

Tools the staff can run themselves, in plain language, documented. No lock-in, no subscription trap, no dependence on me. If I disappeared, everything keeps working. That's the point.

08 The record Why trust me with your team

The discovery method already runs on real people.

The Phase 1 conversation isn't improvised. I've built a working practice around exactly it: profiling a real person by what they do, how comfortable they are with technology, and what they care about — then mapping genuinely useful, no-code tools onto their actual workflow.

I've done this for a foundation executive director, a literary scholar, a commercial producer, an academic-publishing marketer, and a biotech attorney — each non-technical, each handed a short, specific set of things AI could build for them, in their world. A nonprofit's staff is simply the same exercise, run across one team, with working prototypes at the end instead of recommendations.

The bias throughout: small and opinionated over big and generic. Cheap-to-run over subscription-heavy. Local and private when the data is sensitive. Built to be handed off, not held hostage.

09 On trust The part hesitant teams ask about first

The guardrails are the point, not the fine print.

Your people stay in the loop

Tools draft, sort, and suggest. A human always decides. Nothing goes out the door, or into a record, without a person who chose it.

Sensitive data can stay local

For client records, donor details, or anything confidential, tools can run on your own machines and never send private data to an outside service.

You own everything

Every tool, its code, and its documentation are yours to keep, run, and change. No lock-in, no platform you're forced to keep paying.

Honest about the limits

Where AI is unreliable for your task, I'll say so and we won't ship it. Numbers tied to your mission get verified against the source before anyone sees them.

No one gets automated away

The premise is augmentation. Every build targets the drudgery, never the judgment, relationships, or care that only your staff provide.

Built for the budget you have

Default to free and low-cost tooling. The goal is to give hours and capacity back to the mission, not to add a recurring expense.
